## Page 1 — The Story

### About Trakų Jojimo Klubas

[PHOTO 1: Wide shot of TJK arena — early morning, soft light, empty arena. Editorial composition, NE selfie/snapshot.]

Trakų Jojimo Klubas is a riding club and livery yard located 28km west of Vilnius, Lithuania. We operate:

- **18 horses** — 12 owned by the club, 6 on full livery
- **3 instructors** including the founder
- **~40 active clients** — riding school students (24), livery owners (6), occasional visitors (10)
- **~110 lessons per month** in season (May–October), ~70/month in winter
- **One owner-operator** (Andreja Daranda) handling daily operations + yard management + business decisions

This is a mid-sized European riding club — operationally complex enough that informal tools break, small enough that enterprise software is overkill. **It is also our exact ICP for Longrein.**

### The five-tool problem (before Longrein)

For four years, we ran TJK on the same operational stack as most stables our size:

- **Schedule:** A WhatsApp group with three trainers + one Excel sheet that I updated on Sundays.
- **Client roster:** A Google Doc with names, phone numbers, package balances handwritten next to each entry.
- **Horse health:** A paper diary in the office. Vaccination dates, farrier visits, vet notes — all there if you knew which page.
- **Invoicing:** End-of-month batch in Word, exported to PDF, emailed individually. Roughly 4 hours every month, every month.
- **Workload tracking:** None. The closest thing was my own memory: "Caspar feels tired this week." Sometimes I was right. Sometimes I wasn't.

The five tools sort of worked. But they hemorrhaged value:

- I forgot to invoice services that were rendered. Best estimate: **€[TBD]/month in unbilled lessons** that I never recovered.
- I double-booked horses twice in 2025 — clients showed up to ride a horse already in another lesson. Lost two regular clients over it.
- One overworked horse in February pulled out for two weeks of recovery. Lost roughly **€[TBD] in canceled lessons** during that period.
- Sunday evenings became invoicing time. **Roughly 4 hours/week** of admin overhead I was not getting paid for.

### Why I built Longrein

[PHOTO 2: Andreja at TJK, mid-action — talking to instructor, walking through arena, holding tablet. NE staged headshot — editorial documentary style.]

I looked at three pieces of software in 2025: Equine Genie, Stablebuzz, and Barnmanager. None fit. Two reasons:

1. **None of them were built in Europe.** Their pricing was in dollars, their templates assumed US tax categories, their support team was 8 hours behind my time zone, none had SEPA or GDPR baked in.

2. **None of them led with welfare.** Welfare lived under "Reports" — a tab you'd dig into once a quarter, if at all. For me, welfare is the daily question. Which horse is approaching cap? Which one needs a rest week? That should live at the top of the dashboard, not in a report nobody opens.

So I started writing what I needed. With a small dev team. Specifically for stables our size, in our part of the world. Six months later: **Longrein**.

### What Longrein does at TJK today

[PHOTO 3: Tablet on tack box, Longrein dashboard visible. Ambient light, soft focus.]

Every morning at 06:47 (yes, exactly — I check the welfare strip while the kettle boils), Longrein gives me:

- **Welfare bucket strip** — 18 horses sorted into 5 buckets (Over cap / Near cap / Resting / Steady / Light). Anyone in red is one click away.
- **Today's calendar** — 4–6 lessons by trainer, by horse, by client. Drag to reschedule from anywhere.
- **Outstanding balances** — clients with unpaid lessons or overdue boarding. One tap to send a reminder.
- **Recent activity** — last vet visit, last farrier round, last health record per horse.

Every Sunday evening, instead of 4 hours of invoicing, I spend **15 minutes reviewing Longrein's auto-generated invoices** and approving them for send. Stripe handles the SEPA collections.

### The wedge that mattered most

[PHOTO 4: Close-up of a horse — Caspar specifically (founder's horse), or his stall plate. Tight crop, premium feel.]

In late June, our gelding **Caspar** was approaching workload cap. The Longrein strip flagged him in the "Near cap" bucket — 92% of recommended weekly minutes saddled.

Six months earlier, this would have gone unnoticed. Caspar would have been booked into another lesson on Friday. He would have shown up tired, performed poorly, possibly developed a soft tissue strain over the next 10 days. We would have lost him for 2–3 weeks of lessons.

Instead, Longrein flagged it on Monday morning. We rescheduled Friday's lesson to a different horse. Caspar got 4 days of light turnout. By the following week, he was back to "Steady" bucket.

**That single moment paid for the year of Longrein subscription twelve times over.**
