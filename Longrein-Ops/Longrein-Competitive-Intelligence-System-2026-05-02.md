## Dalis 1 — Why competitive monitoring matters (and what it doesn't)

### Why it matters

1. **Avoid blindspots.** Competitor launches your wedge feature → you find out 6 months late from a customer.
2. **Pricing benchmark.** Their price changes signal market dynamics.
3. **Marketing positioning.** Their messaging shifts indicate what they think wins.
4. **Geographic moves.** Equiwise hires DE rep in Latvia? Signal they're entering Baltic market.
5. **Hiring signals.** Series A funding → they'll hire. Hiring DE customer success → DACH expansion. LinkedIn is open intelligence.
6. **Customer migration patterns.** „Why did you leave Stablebuzz?" answers shape your sales pitch.

### What it doesn't matter for

1. **Don't copy features.** Build for YOUR customers, not in reaction to competitors.
2. **Don't compete on price (downward).** Race to bottom = losing strategy in vertical SaaS.
3. **Don't bash competitors publicly.** Brand damage > short-term marketing benefit.
4. **Don't obsess.** Founder time on competitor analysis is time NOT on customers.

### Healthy posture

„I know what they're doing. I don't care about it. I focus on what my customers need."

Quarterly check = healthy awareness. Daily check = obsession (and bad).

## Dalis 5 — Quarterly review template

Block 3 hours every quarter. First Pirmadienis Apr/Jul/Oct/Jan, 09:00-12:00.

### Pre-review (15 min)

- Open Notion „Competitive Intelligence" page
- Pull last quarter's notes (changes baseline)
- Create new Q[X] [Year] entry

### Per-competitor checklist (~15 min each × 7 = 1h 45 min)

For each Tier 1 + Tier 2 competitor (5 total):

#### Website check (5 min)
- Visit competitor.com
- Check pricing page (any changes?)
- Check features page (new features?)
- Check About page (new team members?)
- Check Blog (recent posts indicate priorities?)
- Note URL changes, design changes, copy changes

#### LinkedIn check (5 min)
- Visit competitor LinkedIn Company Page
- Check „People" tab — new hires? (especially DE/LT/PL roles)
- Check Posts — what are they marketing?
- Check founder personal LinkedIn

#### Press mentions check (5 min)
- Google: „[Competitor name] 2026" (last 30 days)
- Industry press: St. Georg, Reiter Revue, Pferdesport (DE)
- LT press: 15min, Verslo žinios (rare for them but check)

### Per-competitor entry format

```
## Q[X] 2026 — [Competitor Name]

### Pricing changes
- (none) OR specific change

### Feature releases
- (none) OR new features added/removed

### Marketing positioning shifts
- (none) OR new messaging/tagline/wedge claims

### Hiring signals (LinkedIn)
- (none) OR roles posted, new hires

### Funding moves
- (none) OR raise/acquisition/investor activity

### Customer signals
- Customer wins (announcements, case studies)
- Customer losses (Reddit/forum complaints, reviews)

### Threat level change
- Same OR Increased OR Decreased
- Reasoning: ___

### Action required
- (none) OR specific tactical response
```

### Cross-cutting analysis (30 min after individual reviews)

Key questions:
1. Any competitor making a major move (funding, geographic expansion, executive hire)?
2. Any pricing trends across the market (going up, going down)?
3. Any feature trends (everyone shipping welfare? Everyone shipping AI?)
4. Any market consolidation signals (acquisitions, partnerships)?
5. What's our UNIQUE position right now? (Should be clear after review.)

### Action items extraction (15 min)

For each competitive signal that requires response:

```
Signal: [What did competitor do]
Risk to Longrein: [How does this affect us]
Response: [Specific action — DO, NOT „watch")
Owner: Andreja
Due: [Date within 30 days]
```

Most quarters → 0-2 action items. If 3+, something strategic is shifting.

## Dalis 7 — Response framework — when competitor moves aggressively

### Scenario A: Competitor launches your wedge feature

Example: Equiwise releases „workload tracker."

**Wrong response:** Panic. Add 5 features in response. Lose focus.

**Right response:**
1. Diagnose: how DEEP is their implementation? (Marketing claim vs production-grade feature?)
2. Talk to YOUR customers: do any plan to switch? If yes, why?
3. Reinforce YOUR moat: blog post, customer testimonials, depth-of-feature comparison
4. Continue product roadmap unchanged

**Time investment:** 4-8 hours research + content. Not weeks of scrambling.

### Scenario B: Competitor cuts pricing aggressively

Example: Stablebuzz drops to $15/mo flat.

**Wrong response:** Match their price. Trash your unit economics.

**Right response:**
1. Acknowledge: their pricing IS more aggressive
2. Reframe value: „We charge €3/horse because we deliver €15-30/horse value (welfare + EU compliance + founder support)"
3. Test: do you actually lose customers to them? Most won't switch over €5/mo if value clear.
4. Don't change pricing reactively. Pricing changes weeks of work.

### Scenario C: Competitor enters your market

Example: Equiwise hires DE rep specifically in Lithuania.

**Wrong response:** Panic. Try to be everywhere they are.

**Right response:**
1. Recognize the validation: „Our market matters."
2. Double down on home base: LT customer success, LSF partnership, content
3. Differentiate where they can't follow: founder-from-here, welfare-first, EU-pan position
4. Speed: ship product faster, close customers faster

### Scenario D: Competitor announces partnership / acquisition

Example: Equiwise acquired by Horse&Country PLC (hypothetical).

**Wrong response:** Speculate publicly. Pivot strategy.

**Right response:**
1. Wait 30 days. Most acquisitions disrupt the acquired company short-term.
2. Watch for customer dissatisfaction (former Equiwise customers may switch).
3. Continue normal customer acquisition.
4. Quietly reach out to former Equiwise customers in your network — see if they're considering alternatives.

### Scenario E: Competitor receives major funding round

Example: Equine Genie raises $20M Series B.

**Wrong response:** Panic about being outspent on marketing.

**Right response:**
1. Funded competitors often expand TAM faster (they ENTER markets you weren't planning for, gives you SOM space at home)
2. Continue your bootstrap discipline
3. Use their fundraise news as your differentiator: „Bootstrap built for stables, not for investor returns"
4. Watch their hiring (signal of where they're investing)

## Dalis 8 — When to ACTIVELY engage competitors

Generally: don't.

But occasionally OK:
- **Public industry forum response:** if competitor makes false public claim about your product, brief factual rebuttal
- **Customer comparison conversation:** if customer asks „how do you compare to X", give honest 2-paragraph differentiation
- **Industry event:** if you're both at LSF conference, polite professional acknowledgment
- **Acquihire / partnership exploration:** if competitor struggling, occasional acquisition discussion (only at specific signals)

NEVER:
- Bash them publicly on social media
- Try to poach their employees aggressively (small industry, reputation matters)
- Submit fake reviews of their product
- Spread rumors about their financial state

## Dalis 10 — Common competitive intelligence mistakes

1. **Daily competitive checks** — turns into obsession, takes time from customers
2. **Pivoting strategy reactively** — competitive moves are signals, not orders
3. **Copying competitor features wholesale** — dilutes your wedge
4. **Public competitor bashing** — damages your brand more than theirs
5. **Ignoring competitors entirely** — you'll be blindsided
6. **Trusting competitor marketing claims** — they overpromise like everyone
7. **Trying to be everywhere they are** — focus matters, especially for solo founders
8. **NOT documenting findings** — quarter X learning forgotten by quarter X+1

## Dalis 11 — Honest assessment

Competitive intelligence for vertical SaaS Solo founder = nice to have, not must-have.

The most successful vertical SaaS founders spend ≤5% of time on competitor analysis and ≥95% on customer success. Why:
- Vertical markets reward depth, not breadth
- Customer NPS > beating competitor in feature comparisons
- Bootstrap'erio time is precious

This document gives you a STRUCTURE so when you DO check competitors (quarterly), it's efficient. NOT a system for daily monitoring obsession.

If you find yourself competing-checking >5 hrs/quarter, you're doing it wrong. Refocus on YOUR customers, YOUR product, YOUR market.
