## Brand voice'o checklist (kiekvienam post'ui)

LinkedIn skirtingai nuo IG:
- **Long-form OK** (300–800 žodžių per post'ą — algoritmas vertina dwell time)
- **No hashtag spam** (3 max — LinkedIn ranks low for hashtag-heavy posts)
- **Direct, professional, but personal** (founder voice'as, ne corporate)
- **Stories > frameworks** (žmonės atsakomi „aha" kai jie atpažįsta save situacijoje)
- **No emojis** arba labai retai (max 1 per post'as)
- **Polls ir questions** veikia (engagement boosters)

Bendras filtras: prieš publish, paklausti — „Ar tai pasakytume su pasitikėjimu užimant publishing slot'ą Forbes Lithuania op-ed sekcijoj?" Jei taip — publish. Jei ne — perdaryti.

## Post #3 — DIENA 5 (Penktadienis, 9 May, 09:00) — Long-form thought leadership

**Format:** ~500 words, no image. Industry POV.

```
A note about the European stable management software landscape.

There are about 14,000 commercial stables in our target geography (Baltics + Poland + DACH). Of those, maybe 3,000 are professional operations with 15+ horses, multiple trainers, recurring clients, regular invoicing.

That is the addressable market for vertical SaaS in this space. By US market terms, it is small.

By US market terms, that is also why nobody has built proper European software for it.

The largest competitors today (Equine Genie, Stablebuzz) are American, priced in dollars, support hours offset to US time, no SEPA, no GDPR-native compliance, English-only. They are not bad products. They are not built for here.

There are three honest paths for stables in our region:
1. Wait for the Americans to localize. They probably will not — the market is too small to be a priority.
2. Build it themselves, on Excel and WhatsApp. Most do this.
3. Use European software made for them.

I am building option 3.

The math on this is not venture-scale. Best case, we serve 1,000–2,000 European stables in five years. That is a 10–25 million euro ARR business. Not a unicorn. A real business.

Vertical SaaS in narrow geographies is unfashionable in Silicon Valley. It is also exactly what most non-US founders should be building. The people who actually need software in your country, in your language, for your operations — they are mostly underserved by the global stack.

Longrein is one example. There are dozens of vertical-and-local SaaS opportunities in the Baltics and Poland and DACH that go unaddressed because the founders comparing themselves to Stripe and Notion think „too small."

It is not too small. It is the actual size that matters here.

Built in Europe, for Europe. That is the wedge.

— Andreja
```

**Why this post works:** Industry analysis + personal POV. Will get reposts from EU SaaS founder community + investors looking for vertical-niche bets.

## Post #5 — DIENA 9 (Antradienis, 13 May, 09:00) — Long-form lesson learned

**Format:** ~450 words, single image (screenshot of welfare bucket strip).

```
Here is a thing that surprised me about building product for our own yard.

When we first designed the welfare workload tracker, the design team's instinct was to put it under "Reports" — a tab you visit when you want to. Most software does it that way. Welfare data is data; data goes in reports.

I overruled it. The strip lives at the top of the dashboard. Five buckets. Twenty-four horses sorted in real time. You see it before you see anything else.

The design team thought I was being overprotective.

In the first three weeks of dogfooding at Trakų Jojimo Klubas, I caught two horses approaching cap that I would have otherwise missed. One I rested for a week. The other I shifted to lower-intensity work for ten days. Both are back to full schedule now. Neither was worked into a soft tissue strain.

Counterfactual: in the version where welfare lives under "Reports," I would not have opened that tab on a Tuesday morning. I would have opened it the following Sunday when I was doing my weekly review. By then both horses would have logged another four lessons each. Statistically, one of them is now out for two to three weeks.

The lesson: information that needs to drive a daily decision needs to live where you make daily decisions. Not where you make weekly reviews.

For stable software specifically, that means: welfare workload at the top of the screen, before the calendar, before the invoice queue, before everything else. Not because it is the most frequently used feature (it is not — the calendar is). Because it is the feature with the highest cost of being missed.

We were wrong to consider putting it under "Reports." We are right to have it where it is.

This is the kind of decision you can only get right by using the product yourself, every day, with stakes. Not in a usability lab. Not in a customer interview. In an actual stable, where if you mis-design the welfare strip, an actual horse pays.

Founder-built vertical SaaS has this advantage. It also has the discipline cost: I have to use Longrein every single day at TJK or I lose the right to make these decisions.

— Andreja, dogfooding Longrein at TJK
```

## Post #6 — DIENA 11 (Ketvirtadienis, 15 May, 09:00) — Industry observation

**Format:** ~250 words. Engaging hook.

```
Three things I have heard from European stable owners in the last month that no American competitor has heard:

1. "I would love proper invoicing — but my accountant uses Rivilė. If your software does not export to Rivilė, I cannot use it."

2. "We need to keep all our client data inside the EU. Our clients are professionals — they ask about GDPR before they sign up for boarding."

3. "Can you set it up so a delayed payment automatically generates a SEPA Direct Debit retry, not a credit card retry? We do not collect cards in our country."

These are not minor preferences. These are the difference between a tool you use and a tool you do not.

The market has been waiting for European SaaS that takes these constraints seriously enough to build for them from scratch. Rivilė integration. EU-only data residency. SEPA-first payment flows. Local-language UI from day one.

Building these as afterthoughts on top of US-built software is hard. Building them in from day one is just product work.

Longrein is one example. There will be others. Every vertical that has been "too small" for US SaaS is suddenly the right size for European builders to address properly.

— Andreja
```

## Post #11 — DIENA 26 (Penktadienis, 30 May, 09:00) — Long-form: industry POV

**Format:** ~600 words. Heavier think piece.

```
I have been thinking about why vertical SaaS for "small" markets gets built so rarely.

The TAM math goes like this. If you are an ambitious founder, your reasonable target is a 100M ARR business or larger. To get to 100M ARR, you need a market with 50,000+ potential customers (assuming 10% penetration, 20K average ACV). The math requires markets like e-commerce SMBs (millions globally) or developer tools (millions globally).

European stable management has 15,000 customers TAM. By that math, the maximum business is maybe 10M ARR. The reasonable business is 5M ARR. By US ambitions, that is "lifestyle" or "subscale."

Here is what that math misses.

A 5M ARR vertical SaaS business in a country like Lithuania or Latvia is among the largest software companies in that country's domestic market. The founder is one of the leading figures in their industry. The product becomes the de facto standard for an entire profession.

Compare to: building a feature for a 100M ARR US horizontal SaaS, where you are one of 200 product managers, your output is a tile in a settings panel, and the company will not remember your contribution in five years.

Where would you rather build?

The Silicon Valley framing of "TAM determines whether to build" is true if your only goal is to build a unicorn. If your goal is to build something that matters in the lives of the people you build it for — TAM is a much weaker filter.

Longrein will probably never be a unicorn. We have done the honest math: 1,000–2,000 stables in Europe, 5M ARR ceiling within five years. We are fine with that. We will be one of the most consequential software products in our customers' working lives.

This logic generalizes. There are dozens of vertical SaaS opportunities in non-US markets that go unbuilt because the founders who could build them have been told the TAM is too small.

— Vintage car dealerships in Italy
— Beekeepers in Slovenia
— Fishery cooperatives in Norway
— Independent pharmacies in Portugal
— Forest landowners in Estonia

Each of these is a 50–500K customer market locally. Each one has a real business waiting to be built. Each one is currently stitched together with WhatsApp and Excel because no one decided to be the company that serves them properly.

The next decade of European tech will not be about building US-style horizontal SaaS that competes with US incumbents.

It will be about building vertical-and-local SaaS that no US founder will ever build, because the US founder thinks the TAM is too small, and they are right that it is too small for them.

It is not too small for us.

— Andreja
Founder, Longrein (vertical SaaS, European stables)
```

**Why this post works:** Has high reshare potential among EU SaaS founders, investors looking at vertical-niche, journalists who write about EU tech. May go semi-viral within EU SaaS LinkedIn community.

# Engagement playbook (LinkedIn-specific)

LinkedIn algorithm rewards conversations more than posts. Daily 30-min commitment:

**Each day:**
1. Reply to ALL comments on YOUR posts within 4 hours (algorithm signal)
2. Comment thoughtfully (3+ sentences) on 5 EU SaaS founder posts
3. Comment on 2 equestrian-industry posts
4. DM 1–2 connection requests with personal note (NOT pitch)
5. React to relevant posts in your network (not spam — only actual interesting stuff)

**Each week:**
- Audit which post performed best (impressions > engagement rate > shares > saves)
- Note pattern — short observations vs long-form vs polls
- Adjust next week based on signal

**Each month:**
- Review network growth (target 200+ new followers/mo)
- Identify 5 new potential connections (industry leaders, federation reps, journalists)
- Send personalized connection requests

**Critical: NEVER**
- Use LinkedIn's „suggested connection" mass invites (low-quality network)
- Post „I'm so excited to announce..." (corporate cringe)
- Use „🚀" or other rocket emoji (LinkedIn's worst cliche)
- Tag random influencers hoping for amplification (transparent and pathetic)
- Auto-DM new connections with sales pitches (immediate unfollow)
